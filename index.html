About Bell Green

IMAGE HERE

Bell Green is an area in south-east London, in the borough of Lewisham. Sandwiched between Forest Hill, Sydenham, Catford and Bellingham, partitioned by a river and railway line and a short walk away from the Borough of Bromley, It is a forgotten land, mostly taken up with a poorly planned retail Park, originally solely occupied by Sainbury's, before further development.


The site of this retail park was once a gas works, generating and supplying gas for heating and lighting. So the land is reasonably toxic, though mostly locked away under the car parks and buildings. Any garden areas are sealed off and there are testing pipes dotted all around the area, for regular environmental sampling.

Because of the retail park, long established crossing point under the railway and relatively poor transport links, Bell Green suffers from large numbers of vehicle journeys, to and through the area, while pedestrians and cyclists have been sidelined, in favour of trying to keep the traffic flowing.


All over London, retail parks are being converted to capitalise on the clamour for more homes to be built, with 'mixed use' regeneration being the preferred model. Considering redevelopment of the retail park is a likely outcome, the community need to be able to decide what sort of redevelopment they want and the area needs, creating a masterplan to guide future planning applications.

Litter

IMAGE HERE

Like many urban places, Bell Green has a littering problem. Litter had been allowed to build up in public and private places and it often appeared that it was not shifting for extended periods of time. A glance under bushes or nehind utility boxes revealed years of historic litter, out of sight, but present.


In 2013, we started picking up litter on our journey to and from school, collecting bags of litter a day from the pavements and gutters. If we saw flytipping, it was reported tot he council for quick removal. We also started tackling really bad areas, getting into bushes, housing estates, privately owned land and public areas that were showing signs of real neglect. Gradually we started to clear the historic litter, whilst also keeping the pavements cleared daily.

What we have noticed, after over a year of action, is that littering seems to have dramatically reduced and the area has changed from one of gathering litter, into one where the pavements and green spaces are often virtually litter free. Spaces behind walls and bushes where bottles and cans were regularly dumped are often completely clear. The main problems now are on private land, animal attacks on street bins and council estate communal bins.


The ultimate aim is not to have to pick up litter at all. We all pay council tax and our local authority and businesses have duties to tackle littering. Changes need to come. New bins, better street cleaning, improved waste management and action to bring business on board to maintain the land around their premises. But until then, repeatedly tackling the litter is the main strategy.

Traffic

IMAGE HERE

Bell Green is an area which is partially locked in. Just to the east is a railway line running north to south, and there are very few crossing points available for road traffic, pedestrians and cyclists. Bell Green hosts one of them. Understandably, this means Bell Green sees a great deal more traffic than other areas, as it is concentrated through the pinch point under the railway line.


This pinch point is very narrow, providing room for two lanes of traffic and only one narrow pavement. It is also low, to the point that it cannot be used by double decker buses.
